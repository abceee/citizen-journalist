Wrapping up & extras

Humanitarian issues are as far-reaching as the extent that technological advancement could take us in the world of communications.

Anyone who writes about disasters and crisis cannot be confined to a set number of coverage guidelines.

But as the global public relies more on citizen journalists for information, their responsibility to provide readers with accurate and informative stories is crucial and unavoidable.

This is another way for citizen journalists to step in to fill the void left by traditional media, and step up to take on the challenging role of journalism especially if you speak the language and familiar with local politics and culture.

This online tutorial has tackled some general writing ideas and pointers. It’s up to you to discover more avenues to tell your story to the world.

Let's recap the main learning points:

  • Asking the right questions fetch meaningful answers and help your story get on track
  • Weigh up all possible causes of a disaster or a humanitarian crisis
  • Go beyond the numbers - use story angles that capture the hearts and minds of your readers
  • Like any news or feature, a story has to be supported by facts with the proper attribution to sources
  • Turn to reliable sources of information such as humanitarian agencies and humanitarian news websites
  • Draw in your audience with story angles that connect to them
  • Look around your community. There could be issues that need to be told
  • Take advantage of gaps in media coverage of long-running and chronic issues
  • Above all, be a responsible citizen journalist! Keep that in mind, as the power of information lies (literally) at your fingertips.

Extras: Adding colour to your story

Do remember that feature stories give any writer the flexibility to be more colourful in style to make the substance of the story come out more. Here are a few basic tips:

  • Be descriptive

    Connect with your readers and make them feel like they are right there in the situation by painting a vivid description of the scenes. Characterise the individuals involved in the story by describing them and the emotions they are showing.

  • Use meaningful quotes

    This helps readers get a real sense of the experience and liven up your story.

  • Use analogies and figures of speech

    Used appropriately and you can effectively explain complex situations to your readers.

  • Don't keep your readers hanging

    End your story well. Either give it a sense of unity by closing with the same idea your story started out with, or give readers something to think about in the end.

    When possible, end on a positive note, keeping in mind that not all humanitarian issues are about gloom and doom.
