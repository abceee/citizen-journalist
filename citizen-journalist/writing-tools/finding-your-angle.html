Finding your angle

Giving your story a good angle draws in your audience.

More importantly, it can also put a much-needed spotlight on long-drawn issues or a crisis slowly unfolding — such as the impact of conflicts, poverty or health risks — which mainstream media could not cover either due to lack of journalists specialising on humanitarian stories or that chronic issues tend to lose in the fight for editorial priority as the headlines keep changing.

Humanising your story

Put a face to the people behind the numbers

There is more to writing humanitarian features than just laying out facts and figures. The key is to get the public’s attention to the ‘human’ in ‘humanitarian’.

Telling the story through the eyes of individuals will allow your readers to see the big picture.

In the initial hours following a disaster like an earthquake, the first responders are in many cases the victims themselves.

Get to know the grassroots and find connections around you

Websites of aid organisations and humanitarian news services feature surveys on various issues and profiles of countries particularly prone to humanitarian crisis.

Again go beyond the figures, as these resources can help you better understand people’s backgrounds, cultures and beliefs that could in part explain their plight.

Look around you: What humanitarian issue do you feel the world should know more about? Connect with your community and see what you can uncover.

Anniversary stories and keeping track of progress

A disaster or crisis is put back into the spotlight usually a few months after it took place and followed up a year after. It poses questions such as:

  • How are survivors coping and recovering? What about those who lost loved ones?
  • What has been done to improve on the early warning system such as in the case of a tsunami or flooding disaster?
  • Aid agencies are not only there for relief efforts. They can help residents become more resilient. How have they helped residents in that regard?

Sample Situation: In a Courier Mail article on Cyclone Yasi’s post-devastation of north Queensland entitled “Six months after Cyclone Yasi, heartbreak remains”, it highlights the long wait for thousands of affected people to get replies to their insurance claims.

Offbeat & off-stage

Offbeat angles lighten the touch of the story

Humanitarian stories are not always about tragedy and misery

Donkeys coming to the rescue of Pakistanis affected by massive flooding in 2010 to bring relief supplies is an example of an offbeat story. Or, locals tapping into elephants to clear up debris following the Indian Ocean tsunami in 2004.

Stories like these highlight the resourcefulness of people particularly in times of crisis.

Drawing on ‘star’ power

Off the stage and outside the glitter of show business, more and more celebrities are joining the humanitarian cause and are making headlines because of this.

But there is a tendency for the coverage to either be short-term or that the celebrity becomes the focus of stories rather than the issue.
